\section{Introduction and Overview}
% =============================================================

This template introduces all the structural and typographic features
available in the Trans/Act submission format. Each section below
demonstrates a specific feature and explains how it is handled across
the three outputs: the compiled PDF you produce locally, the platform HTML
article, and the downloadable PDF generated by the platform.

Write your manuscript in standard LaTeX. The platform's parser reads your
\texttt{.tex} source and produces a canonical structured representation
that drives both the HTML and PDF outputs. You do not need to worry about
platform-specific formatting: use standard LaTeX commands and the custom
macros defined in \texttt{arjournal.cls}.

Artistic research and multimedia scholarship have developed robust
conventions for integrating practice and writing \citep{smith2024art}.
Practice-based methods require particular care when moving between media
and text \citep{brown2022practice}. \citet{borgdorff2012conflict} argues
that artistic research occupies a contested space between studio practice
and academic convention---a tension this format is designed to accommodate.
This template ensures your submission is correctly structured for all three
outputs from a single source.

\section{Headings and Document Structure}
% =============================================================

Use \verb|\section{...}| for top-level headings and
\verb|\subsection{...}| for second-level headings. Avoid going deeper
than \verb|\subsubsection{...}| — the platform supports three levels.
Unnumbered sections (\verb|\section*{...}|) are also supported.

\subsection{Numbered Subsections}

Subsections are numbered automatically. The platform extracts headings to
build a table of contents displayed in the article sidebar.

\subsection{Avoid Manual Spacing}

Do not insert \verb|\vspace|, \verb|\newpage|, or manual line breaks to
control layout: the platform generates its own layout from the parsed
structure. Manual spacing is ignored during parsing.

\section{Inline Text Formatting}
% =============================================================

Standard LaTeX inline markup is fully supported and preserved in the HTML
and PDF outputs.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bold text} is produced with \verb|\textbf{...}|.
  \item \textit{Italic text} is produced with \verb|\textit{...}| or \verb|\emph{...}|.
  \item Both may be combined: \textbf{\textit{bold italic}}.
  \item Hyperlinks: \verb|\url{https://example.org}| produces \url{https://example.org}.
  \item Named links: \verb|\href{url}{text}| produces \href{https://example.org}{a clickable link}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Quotations}
% =============================================================

The platform distinguishes between two quotation styles based on length.

\subsection{Short Quotations (under 40 words / 4 lines)}

Short quotations are embedded \emph{within} the running paragraph text,
enclosed in typographic double quotation marks. Use \verb|\enquote{...}|
from the \texttt{csquotes} package (loaded by \texttt{arjournal.cls}):

\begin{verbatim}
As the artist noted, \enquote{the body is the first instrument of research,
prior to all notation and inscription}.
\end{verbatim}

Rendered inline: As the artist noted, \enquote{the body is the first instrument
of research, prior to all notation and inscription}. The platform converts
\verb|\enquote{...}| to proper Unicode curly quotation marks (\textquotedblleft\ldots\textquotedblright)
in both HTML and PDF.

You may also write inline quotes using the \verb|\ARJquote{...}| alias,
which behaves identically:

\begin{verbatim}
\ARJquote{practice precedes its own theorisation}
\end{verbatim}

Output: \ARJquote{practice precedes its own theorisation}.

\subsection{Long Quotations (40 words or more / 4 or more lines)}

Extended quotations are set as freestanding blocks, indented on both
sides, without quotation marks. Use the \texttt{ARJblockquote} environment
(or the standard LaTeX \texttt{quote} environment—both are handled
identically by the parser):

\begin{verbatim}
\begin{ARJblockquote}
Artistic research differs from the artistic practice on which it is
based in that it aims at producing communicable insights. The specific
contribution of artistic research is a form of knowledge that is
inseparable from the practices through which it is produced—knowledge
that resists full translation into propositional language and must
be encountered in its material, temporal, or performative form.
\end{ARJblockquote}
\end{verbatim}

The result:

\begin{ARJblockquote}
Artistic research differs from the artistic practice on which it is
based in that it aims at producing communicable insights. The specific
contribution of artistic research is a form of knowledge that is
inseparable from the practices through which it is produced—knowledge
that resists full translation into propositional language and must
be encountered in its material, temporal, or performative form.
\end{ARJblockquote}

The passage above is adapted from \citet[p.~44]{borgdorff2012conflict}.
When quoting a source at this length, include the page number in your
citation using \verb|\citep[p.~44]{key}|.

In the \textbf{PDF} the block is indented 0.5~inches (36~pt) on both
sides and set in italic. In the \textbf{HTML} article it appears as a
\texttt{<blockquote>} element, styled with a left border and reduced
font size.

\section{Footnotes}
% =============================================================

Use standard LaTeX \verb|\footnote{...}| for all supplementary notes.

\begin{verbatim}
The gesture was recorded in 2019.\footnote{The full video documentation
is archived at the institution's media repository, accession 2019-AR-011.}
\end{verbatim}

Rendered: The gesture was recorded in 2019.\footnote{The full video
documentation is archived at the institution's media repository, accession
2019-AR-011. For a broader discussion of embodied knowledge and digital
archives, see \citet{andersen2022embodied}.}

\textbf{In the PDF} footnotes appear at the \emph{bottom of the page} on
which they are referenced, numbered sequentially.

\textbf{In the HTML article} footnotes appear as \emph{margin sidenotes}
on the right-hand side, at the same vertical position as their reference
point in the text. On smaller screens (below 1200~px) the sidenotes
collapse and a numbered \enquote{Notes} section is displayed at the end
of the article instead.

You can place multiple footnotes in a single section or paragraph.\footnote{%
  Each footnote receives a unique sequential number. The platform handles
  numbering automatically; do not use manual numbering commands.%
}
Practice-based research often relies on supplementary documentation whose
full detail sits more naturally in a note than in the main text
\citep{nelson2013practice}. Use footnotes for this kind of material rather
than attempting to integrate archival references directly into the argument.

\section{Bibliographic Citations}
% =============================================================

Citations use the \texttt{natbib} package loaded by \texttt{arjournal.cls}.
The default bibliography style is \texttt{apalike} (author–year, APA-like).

\subsection{Citation Commands}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \verb|\citep{key}| — parenthetical citation: \citep{smith2024art}
  \item \verb|\citet{key}| — narrative citation: \citet{brown2022practice}
  \item \verb|\citep[p.~45]{key}| — with page: \citep[p.~45]{smith2024art}
  \item \verb|\citep{key1,key2}| — multiple keys: \citep{smith2024art,brown2022practice}
  \item \verb|\cite{key}| — alias for \verb|\citep{key}| (also supported)
\end{itemize}

In the compiled PDF, natbib produces standard author–year parenthetical
references. In the HTML article, each citation renders as a link
(\texttt{(key)}) that scrolls to the corresponding entry in the
References section at the foot of the article.

\subsection{Managing the Bibliography File}

Store your references in the companion \texttt{.bib} file
(\texttt{arjournal\_references\_template.bib}). The platform extracts
the citation keys referenced in your text; the full bibliography is
compiled from your \texttt{.bib} file using BibTeX.

\begin{verbatim}
\bibliographystyle{apalike}
\bibliography{arjournal_references_template}
\end{verbatim}

Supported BibTeX entry types: \texttt{@article}, \texttt{@book},
\texttt{@incollection}, \texttt{@inproceedings}, \texttt{@online},
\texttt{@misc}, \texttt{@phdthesis}, \texttt{@techreport}.

Sound art scholarship has also addressed research methodologies at
length \citep{lee2023sound}. Online resources and performance
documentation may be cited as \texttt{@online} entries
\citep{video_example}, and datasets as \texttt{@misc} entries
\citep{dataset_example}.

\section{Images}
% =============================================================

Images are central to many artistic research submissions. Visual
documentation---of performances, studio processes, artefacts, or
site-specific work---often carries argumentative weight that cannot be
reduced to text \citep{jones2023practice}. The platform renders images
as full-width HTML figures in the published article and embeds them
inline in the downloadable PDF.

Use \verb|\ARJfigure| for all images. Upload the image file to the
platform asset manager using exactly the filename you reference here.

\begin{verbatim}
\ARJfigure{figure1.jpg}
          {Caption text displayed below the image.}
          {Alt text: a detailed verbal description for accessibility.}
\end{verbatim}

\ARJfigure{example-image-a}{%
  Example figure. Captions should briefly describe the image and its
  relevance to the argument. Include credit or rights information here
  if required by the rights holder.%
}{%
  A grey placeholder rectangle representing an image asset. In the
  published article this will be replaced by the uploaded photograph.%
}

The third argument (alt text) is \emph{required}. It is used by screen
readers and by the platform's accessibility checker. Describe the
image's content and, where relevant, its significance for readers who
cannot see it \citep{sar2021guidelines}.

\subsection{Accepted image formats}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{JPEG} (\texttt{.jpg}, \texttt{.jpeg}) --- \emph{preferred
    for photographs and continuous-tone images}. Use a quality setting of
    at least 85\% (high). Avoid re-saving JPEGs repeatedly; each save
    introduces compression artefacts.
  \item \textbf{PNG} (\texttt{.png}) --- preferred for diagrams, charts,
    screenshots, and images with transparent backgrounds. Lossless
    compression preserves sharp edges and text.
  \item \textbf{WebP} (\texttt{.webp}) --- accepted; offers better
    compression than JPEG or PNG at equivalent quality. Not all PDF
    viewers render WebP correctly; supply a JPEG or PNG fallback if in
    doubt.
  \item \textbf{TIFF} (\texttt{.tif}, \texttt{.tiff}) --- accepted for
    archival-quality originals. TIFF files are converted to JPEG or PNG
    during the production build. Note that TIFF files are often very
    large; check the size limits below.
  \item \textbf{SVG} (\texttt{.svg}) --- accepted for vector graphics,
    diagrams, maps, and data visualisations. Embedded fonts must be
    outlined or included; text that relies on system fonts may not render
    correctly on all platforms.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Not accepted:} BMP, GIF (use PNG instead), PSD, AI, EPS, HEIC/HEIF,
RAW camera formats (\texttt{.cr2}, \texttt{.nef}, \texttt{.arw}, etc.).
Convert RAW files to TIFF or high-quality JPEG before uploading.

\subsection{Resolution and file size}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Minimum resolution:} 150\,dpi at the intended display
    size. Images below this threshold may appear blurry in the HTML
    article.
  \item \textbf{Recommended resolution:} 300\,dpi for photographs and
    figures that readers may wish to zoom in on or download; this also
    ensures acceptable quality in the generated PDF \citep{andersen2022embodied}.
  \item \textbf{Practical pixel dimensions:} for a full-width figure
    (approximately 800\,px on screen), supply an image at least
    1600$\times$900\,px; for a figure intended at half-column width,
    800$\times$600\,px is sufficient.
  \item \textbf{Maximum file size:} \textbf{20\,MB} per image asset.
    Images larger than this will be rejected by the upload form. If your
    original is larger, export a compressed version (JPEG at 85\%+
    quality, or PNG with lossless compression) before uploading.
  \item \textbf{Total asset bundle:} the combined size of all uploaded
    assets (images, video, audio, transcripts) must not exceed
    \textbf{2\,GB} per submission.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Captions, credits, and rights}

Every figure must have a caption (argument~2) and alt text (argument~3).
Captions appear beneath the image in both the HTML article and the PDF.
Include rights and credit information in the caption if required by the
rights holder---for example: \emph{Photograph by Elena Garc\'ia,
\copyright~2024. Reproduced with permission.}

You are responsible for holding or obtaining the rights to all images
you submit. The platform does not verify image rights automatically.

\section{Video}
% =============================================================

Video assets are embedded as HTML5 \texttt{<video>} elements in the
published article. In the locally compiled PDF they appear as a labelled
placeholder (file name, poster image reference, duration); the actual
player is only available in the online version.

\subsection{Accepted video formats}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MP4} (\texttt{.mp4}) with H.264 video and AAC audio ---
    \emph{preferred}; plays natively in all modern browsers without a
    plugin.
  \item \textbf{WebM} (\texttt{.webm}) with VP9 or AV1 --- recommended
    as a second source for open-format compatibility.
  \item \textbf{OGV} (\texttt{.ogv}) with Theora --- accepted but not
    recommended; limited device support.
\end{itemize}

Maximum file size: \textbf{2\,GB} per video asset. For longer
performances or installations, contact the editorial office to arrange
hosted streaming. Resolution should be at least 720p (1280$\times$720);
1080p (1920$\times$1080) is preferred.

\subsection{Macro syntax}

\begin{verbatim}
\ARJvideo{filename.mp4}
         {Caption displayed beneath the video player.}
         {poster.jpg}
         {transcript.vtt}
\end{verbatim}

\begin{description}
  \item[Argument 1] File name of the video asset (include the extension).
    Upload the file through the platform asset manager using exactly this
    name.
  \item[Argument 2] Caption text, displayed below the player in the
    published article.
  \item[Argument 3] File name of the poster image shown before the video
    plays. Accepted formats: JPEG, PNG, or WebP. Recommended size:
    1280$\times$720\,px or 1920$\times$1080\,px.
  \item[Argument 4] File name of the transcript. Accepted transcript
    formats: \texttt{.vtt} (WebVTT, preferred) or \texttt{.txt}
    (plain text). Transcripts are required for accessibility compliance.
\end{description}

\ARJvideo{performance1.mp4}{%
  Performance excerpt showing the central gesture sequence,
  recorded live at the venue. Duration 4:32.%
}{poster1.jpg}{performance1_transcript.vtt}

A cited online video resource (hosted on Vimeo, YouTube, or an
institutional repository) can appear in the bibliography
as an \texttt{@online} entry \citep{video_example}. For the question
of how digital video archives function as research infrastructure,
see \citet{andersen2022embodied}.

\section{Audio}
% =============================================================

Audio assets are embedded as HTML5 \texttt{<audio>} elements in the
published article. In the PDF they appear as a labelled placeholder
with the file name, caption, and duration. Sound-based and
electroacoustic practices increasingly require audio documentation as a
primary research output rather than supplementary material
\citep{lee2023sound}.

\subsection{Accepted audio formats}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MP3} (\texttt{.mp3}) --- \emph{preferred}; universally
    supported.
  \item \textbf{WAV} (\texttt{.wav}) --- accepted for uncompressed
    archival-quality audio; note that file sizes can be large.
  \item \textbf{AAC} (\texttt{.aac}, \texttt{.m4a}) --- accepted;
    good quality-to-size ratio.
  \item \textbf{FLAC} (\texttt{.flac}) --- accepted for lossless
    material; browser support is more limited than MP3.
  \item \textbf{OGG Vorbis} (\texttt{.ogg}) --- accepted; preferred
    open format alongside MP3.
\end{itemize}

Maximum file size: \textbf{500\,MB} per audio asset. Sample rate should
be at least 44.1\,kHz; 48\,kHz is preferred for field recordings and
studio material.

\subsection{Macro syntax}

\begin{verbatim}
\ARJaudio{filename.mp3}
         {Caption displayed beneath the audio player.}
         {transcript.txt}
\end{verbatim}

\begin{description}
  \item[Argument 1] File name of the audio asset (include the extension).
  \item[Argument 2] Caption text, displayed below the player in the
    published article.
  \item[Argument 3] File name of the transcript. Accepted formats:
    \texttt{.vtt} (WebVTT, preferred for timed captions) or
    \texttt{.txt} (plain text). Transcripts are required for
    accessibility compliance.
\end{description}

\ARJaudio{soundscape1.wav}{%
  Soundscape excerpt used in the analysis (02:18).
  Field recording, mixed with archival material.%
}{soundscape1_transcript.txt}

\section{Tables}
% =============================================================

Tables should be authored in standard LaTeX using the \texttt{table}
and \texttt{tabular} environments with \texttt{booktabs} rules
(\verb|\toprule|, \verb|\midrule|, \verb|\bottomrule|). This keeps
the data machine-readable and allows the platform to render it
correctly in both HTML and PDF.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Review criteria and weightings}
\label{tab:criteria}
\begin{tabular}{llr}
\toprule
Criterion       & Description                    & Weight \\
\midrule
Originality     & Novel contribution to practice & 30\%   \\
Rigour          & Methodological quality         & 30\%   \\
Clarity         & Communicative effectiveness    & 20\%   \\
Journal fit     & Relevance to artistic research & 20\%   \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Always supply a \verb|\caption| and a \verb|\label| for every table.
Do not use \verb|\multicolumn| or \verb|\multirow| spanning cells;
the platform's HTML table renderer does not support them. For data that
is too complex for a table, consider whether a figure or an appendix
dataset \citep{dataset_example} would communicate the material more
effectively.

\section{Lists and Bullet Points}
% =============================================================

Standard \texttt{itemize} (bullet list) and \texttt{enumerate}
(numbered list) environments are fully supported. Keep list nesting
to a maximum of \textbf{two levels}; deeper nesting is flattened
to paragraphs during parsing. The Society for Artistic Research
submission guidelines \citep{sar2021guidelines} recommend using lists
sparingly: prefer flowing prose for argumentative content and reserve
lists for genuinely enumerable items (file formats, steps in a
process, criteria).

\subsection{Unordered (bullet) lists}

Use \texttt{itemize} for lists where order is not significant.

\begin{verbatim}
\begin{itemize}
  \item First item.
  \item Second item, with \textbf{inline formatting}.
  \item Third item, with a footnote.\footnote{Footnote text here.}
\end{itemize}
\end{verbatim}

Rendered result:

\begin{itemize}
  \item First item.
  \item Second item, with \textbf{inline formatting}.
  \item Third item, with a footnote.\footnote{%
    This footnote is inside a list item. It appears in the right margin
    of the HTML article and at the foot of the PDF page.%
  }
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Ordered (numbered) lists}

Use \texttt{enumerate} for sequential steps or ranked items.

\begin{verbatim}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item First step.
  \item Second step.
  \item Third step.
\end{enumerate}
\end{verbatim}

Rendered result:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item First step.
  \item Second step.
  \item Third step.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Nested lists (maximum two levels)}

\begin{verbatim}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Top-level item.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Nested item one.
    \item Nested item two.
  \end{itemize}
  \item Another top-level item.
\end{itemize}
\end{verbatim}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Top-level item.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Nested item one.
    \item Nested item two.
  \end{itemize}
  \item Another top-level item.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Description lists}

Use \texttt{description} for term--definition pairs (e.g.\ glossaries,
argument lists).

\begin{verbatim}
\begin{description}
  \item[Term one] Definition or explanation of the first term.
  \item[Term two] Definition or explanation of the second term.
\end{description}
\end{verbatim}

\begin{description}
  \item[Term one] Definition or explanation of the first term.
  \item[Term two] Definition or explanation of the second term.
\end{description}

\section{Complete Workflow Summary}
% =============================================================

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Prepare your manuscript.} Write the article in this
    template. Replace all placeholder text. Fill in the metadata
    commands in the preamble.

  \item \textbf{Manage your bibliography.} Add all references to the
    companion \texttt{.bib} file. Use \verb|\citep{key}| and
    \verb|\citet{key}| throughout the text.

  \item \textbf{Compile locally.} Run \texttt{pdflatex} $\to$
    \texttt{bibtex} $\to$ \texttt{pdflatex} $\to$ \texttt{pdflatex}
    to verify that citations, cross-references, and figures resolve
    correctly.

  \item \textbf{Upload.} Submit the \texttt{.tex} file, the \texttt{.bib}
    file, and all asset files (images, video, audio, transcripts) via
    the journal platform submission portal.

  \item \textbf{Review.} The platform parses your \texttt{.tex} file,
    generates an HTML preview, and routes the submission through the
    editorial and peer-review workflow.

  \item \textbf{Production.} Accepted articles are published as
    interactive HTML and downloadable PDF from the same source,
    following the editorial standards of Trans/Act
    \citep{journalwebsite}.
\end{enumerate}

For an overview of artistic research publication norms across
institutions, see \citet{sar2021guidelines}. For the epistemological
stakes of this kind of dissemination, see \citet{nelson2013practice}
and \citet{white2021choreography}.
